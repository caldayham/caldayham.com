Dan Koe is Lying to You. (and so is everybody else)

October 28, 2025

There are millions of people trying to sell information online because information has no physical volume, but it does have real value. It makes the perfect product online.

What no one wants to tell you is that the best package of information to sell online is how to sell information online. The higher you go up this meta-ladder, the more you can charge, and the better your margins get. It's not exactly a pyramid scheme, but it shares quite a few attributes ;)

The Promise

Here's the standard pitch: Get good at social media. Get good at marketing. Understand how people think. Create a valuable product. Match that product to your market. Build an audience around your niche, create a product around your passion, and make money. Simple, right?

The implication is straightforward: you're going to build this audience, take the thing you love, make a product around it, and earn as much as the people teaching you. But there's a catch that nobody mentions upfront.

The Pyramid Reality

The best products to actually make that kind of money aren't products about your random passion. They're products teaching people how to make products. And it gets better: teaching people how to make courses on how to make courses on how to make courses. Each layer up, you can charge more.

Why? Because someone selling a course can afford to pay more for education than someone just learning a regular skill. A course about writing as a business model can reasonably charge $150 because you're selling a business framework, not just writing tips. But try charging $150 for a course on supplement optimization or woodworking basics. Your market shrinks dramatically because those buyers aren't planning to turn around and sell their own courses.

Twitter and the Race

Scroll through Twitter and you'll see countless bios: "I scaled 4 people to 10,000 followers and I can do the same for you." Click on any profile in this space and you'll find variations of the same thing: selling digital products on how to sell digital products. SMM courses. Creator economy courses. Audience building courses. All roads lead to the same destination.

It starts to look like a race to the bottom, or maybe a race to the top of the pyramid. Use fancy tools to promote fancy courses to grow really fast so you can sell courses on how to sell courses. The system feeds itself.

The Lower Rungs

Let's say you actually care about something tangible. Physical health, for example. You research supplements extensively. You could probably deliver one-fifth the value of an hour with the world's best supplement expert. People might pay $100-200 for that information if it's genuinely useful and saves them time.

But here's the problem: you can't charge $150 for that course, because it's not teaching someone how to start a business that will make them enough money to buy more courses. You're stuck on a lower rung. Your market is people who want to optimize their health, not people who want to build a course-selling business.

The math works differently at different levels. To make money selling practical skills, you need volume. To make money selling business frameworks, you need far fewer customers at higher price points. And to make money selling frameworks about selling frameworks? Now you're playing a different game entirely.

The Nuance Nobody Mentions

The title of this article strips away nuance by design, just like the content it critiques. Dan Koe isn't lying in the traditional sense. The Two-Hour Writer is a solid product. Justin Welsh writes absolutely killer Twitter threads that are genuinely useful. These people are skilled at what they do.

But there's an implication in the creator economy content that's technically true but missing context: "You are the niche. Build an audience around that niche. Create a product. Sell it." The unspoken part is that the most lucrative version of this formula is when your niche is teaching other people to do exactly what you're doing.

It's not wrong. It's just that the stack goes up further than most people realize, and the people at the top have a vested interest in selling shovels during a gold rush without explicitly saying "the real gold is in selling shovels."

Where Does This Leave You?

If you're on a lower rung selling actual skills or products, you're not doomed. You're just playing a different game with different economics. You might need more customers, better margins through unique expertise, or a different path to scale. But you're probably not going to make creator economy money selling supplement advice or woodworking tips.

If you're climbing the meta-ladder, understand what you're doing. Each level up gets you access to customers with more purchasing power because they're using your product as a tool to make money, not just to improve their lives. There's nothing inherently wrong with this, but it's worth acknowledging the structure openly.

The real question isn't whether this is a pyramid scheme (it's not, technically). The real question is which game you want to play and whether you're being honest with yourself about which game you're actually playing.
